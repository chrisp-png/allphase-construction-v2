# Florida Flat Roof Cost 2026: $9K-$22K (TPO, PVC, EPDM Guide)

**Updated May 7, 2026.** Flat roofs are a real category in Florida residential roofing — not just a commercial system pressed into service. Modern architecture, mid-century homes, screened-porch additions, and second-story rooftop decks all rely on flat-roof construction. This guide walks through the four membrane systems used on Florida homes in 2026, the HVHZ rules that govern them in Broward County, the prevailing voluntary HVHZ-spec standard in Palm Beach County, current 2026 cost ranges, and the decision logic for when a flat roof is the right call versus a sloped alternative.

---

## Why flat roofs are different in Florida

A "flat" roof isn't actually flat. Every functional flat roof has a slight slope (typically 1/4 inch per foot, or about 2%) built into the roof deck so water can drain to scuppers, internal drains, or eaves. The Florida twist is that 2% slope on a hot, humid, hurricane-exposed roof is unforgiving: water that ponds for more than 48 hours after rain finds every imperfection in the membrane. Florida flat roofs fail at the seams, at penetrations, and at edge metal long before they fail in the field.

Two regulatory regimes shape what gets installed. **Broward County is a legal High-Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ)** under the Florida Building Code, so every flat-roof system must use a Florida Notice of Acceptance (NOA)–approved membrane, NOA-approved fastening or adhesion method, and NOA-approved edge metal. **Palm Beach County is not legally HVHZ**, but the prevailing standard for residential flat-roof replacement is to install voluntarily to HVHZ specification because the same coastal wind exposure applies. Reputable contractors install the same NOA-approved system on either side of the county line.

## The four flat-roof membrane systems in 2026

### TPO (Thermoplastic Polyolefin) — the current default

TPO has become the default residential flat-roof system in South Florida for new installations. White-surfaced for solar reflectivity (which directly reduces cooling load on a Florida home), heat-welded seams (no adhesive failure mode), and 60-mil membrane thickness as the residential standard. Heat-welded seams are the key advantage: when a TPO seam is welded properly, it's as strong as the membrane itself, and a single bad seam is usually visible at install time rather than failing two years later. **Typical service life on a well-installed Florida TPO roof is 20 to 25 years.**

### PVC (Polyvinyl Chloride) — the upgrade tier

PVC is the higher-grade single-ply system. It outperforms TPO in two specific scenarios: when the rooftop has chemical exposure (kitchen exhaust, restaurant grease venting, pool-equipment chemicals) and when fire rating matters more than baseline. PVC is more expensive than TPO at the same membrane thickness, but the lifespan and chemical resistance justify it for the right application. On a typical residential Florida home without rooftop chemical exposure, TPO is usually the smarter choice; PVC is the right answer on multi-story homes with rooftop kitchens or near restaurant-density commercial corridors.

### EPDM (Ethylene Propylene Diene Monomer rubber) — the legacy system

EPDM is the legacy black-rubber single-ply system. It's still common on existing Florida flat roofs and is well-suited to recover work where the existing system is intact but at end-of-service-life. New EPDM installations have largely given way to TPO except in specific cold-cycle or chemical-exposure applications, neither of which is a meaningful Florida concern. EPDM's black surface absorbs heat, which is a real cooling-cost penalty in Broward and Palm Beach summers — that alone is enough reason to not use EPDM on a new South Florida residential install in 2026.

### Modified bitumen (mod-bit) — the older asphalt-based system

Modified bitumen is the older asphalt-based flat-roof category. Hot-applied or cold-process, applied in two- or three-ply systems, with a granulated cap sheet on top. It's still in service on a large number of South Florida buildings. Modern single-ply systems generally offer easier installation, better seam reliability (heat-welded vs. torch-down), and lighter weight — so when a mod-bit roof reaches end-of-service-life, the typical replacement path in 2026 is a TPO recover with new tapered insulation, rather than another mod-bit application.

## HVHZ rules in plain English

In **Broward County (legal HVHZ)**, every product on the roof needs a Florida Notice of Acceptance (NOA). The permit package references NOA numbers for the membrane, the underlayment (if used), the insulation, the fasteners or adhesive, and the edge metal. A reputable contractor produces NOAs for every component used on your specific job. If a contractor cannot produce NOAs, the roof is not legally permittable in Broward.

In **Palm Beach County (not legally HVHZ but prevailingly built to HVHZ spec)**, the same NOA-approved system is the prevailing standard. The cost difference between an HVHZ-spec install and a non-HVHZ install on a residential flat roof is small relative to total project cost, and the wind exposure is identical, so most reputable Palm Beach contractors install to HVHZ spec by default.

## 2026 cost ranges for residential flat roofs

Pricing reflects Q1 2026 South Florida market rates for HVHZ-spec installation:

- **TPO recover** (over existing intact substrate): roughly $9 to $12 per square foot of flat area.
- **TPO full replacement** (tear-off + new insulation + new membrane): roughly $11 to $14 per square foot.
- **PVC recover**: roughly $11 to $14 per square foot.
- **PVC full replacement**: roughly $13 to $17 per square foot.
- **Modified bitumen recover**: roughly $7 to $10 per square foot (typically only recommended when the existing system is intact and homeowner wants budget option).
- **EPDM recover**: roughly $8 to $11 per square foot.

The biggest cost drivers, in order, are:

1. **Existing condition** — does the deck need replacement, does the existing system need full tear-off, are there ponding-water issues that require new tapered insulation
2. **Membrane thickness** — 60-mil vs 80-mil
3. **Penetrations** — the number and type (drains, scuppers, HVAC curbs, plumbing vents)
4. **Edge-metal detail**

A simple flat-roof recover on a screened-porch addition lands at the bottom of the range; a full tear-off with new tapered insulation and complex penetrations on a multi-level mid-century home lands at the top.

## When a flat roof is the right call (and when to convert to sloped)

Homeowners often ask if they should convert a flat roof to a sloped roof during replacement. The answer in Florida is usually **no**, for three reasons:

- Modern flat-roof membrane systems (TPO and PVC) installed to HVHZ spec have 20 to 25 year service lives, which is comparable to or better than asphalt shingles installed in the same climate.
- Converting flat to sloped is a structural project, not a roofing project. It requires engineered framing, new wall heights, fascia and gutter integration, often new HVAC routing, and almost always a permit-approval process that runs 6 to 12 weeks. Cost is typically 3-5x a like-for-like flat-roof replacement.
- On modern and mid-century architectural styles, the flat roof IS the design — converting it changes the look of the home.

That said, there are scenarios where conversion makes sense: chronic ponding-water issues caused by structural settling that can't be corrected with tapered insulation, repeated leak failures despite proper membrane installation, or a home where the flat-roof element is on a small addition (screened porch, single-room expansion) and the homeowner is also re-roofing the main sloped section. For most homes, though, replacing in kind is the right call.

## Maintenance and what to expect

A properly installed flat roof in Florida needs annual inspection — twice a year is better. The maintenance checklist is short but real:

- Clear debris from drains and scuppers (especially after every storm)
- Inspect seams visually for separation or bubbling
- Check all penetration boots (plumbing vents, HVAC curbs) for cracking
- Verify edge metal is still tight to the deck
- Look for any sign of ponding water that didn't drain within 48 hours of rain

Most issues are caught and fixed at this maintenance level for a few hundred dollars; left ignored, the same issue becomes a leak that requires partial membrane replacement at the cost of a few thousand dollars.

**Most flat-roof manufacturer warranties** (10 to 20 years for TPO and PVC) require documented annual inspections. Skipping the inspections doesn't just risk leaks — it can void the warranty.
