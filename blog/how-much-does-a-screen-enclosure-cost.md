# How Much Does a Screen Enclosure Cost in South Florida?

**The complete 2026 pricing guide for screen enclosures and pool cages in Broward and Palm Beach Counties — including HVHZ requirements that most homeowners don't know about**

---

## TL;DR

**Screen enclosures in South Florida cost $5,000 to $15,000+ depending on size, materials, and building code requirements.** Unlike other parts of Florida, Broward and Palm Beach Counties are High Velocity Hurricane Zones (HVHZ), which means your screen enclosure must withstand 180mph winds — not the standard 140mph. This requires engineered frames, enhanced fastening, and strict permitting. Aluminum frames are standard ($8–$15 per square foot installed), while steel frames cost more but offer superior strength ($12–$20+ per square foot). Pool enclosures require additional safety features. Rescreening existing frames runs $2–$4 per square foot, while full rebuilds start at $10,000 for a typical pool cage. Every screen enclosure project in South Florida requires permits and engineering — anyone offering to skip permits is setting you up for insurance problems and code violations.

## Why Screen Enclosures Cost More in South Florida

If you've talked to friends or family in Central or North Florida, you've probably heard much lower prices.

Here's why South Florida is different:

### 1. High Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ) Requirements

**Most of Broward and Palm Beach Counties are HVHZ.**

This means screen enclosures must be engineered to withstand:

- **180mph wind speeds** (vs 140mph in non-HVHZ areas)
- **Higher wind pressure calculations**
- **Enhanced fastening requirements**
- **Stricter structural engineering**

**This isn't optional.** It's Florida Building Code, and it directly impacts material costs, labor, and engineering fees.

### 2. Engineering Requirements

Every screen enclosure in HVHZ areas requires:

✓ **Structural engineering drawings** — Stamped by a Florida-licensed PE
✓ **Wind load calculations** — Specific to your property
✓ **Foundation specifications** — Proper anchoring to concrete
✓ **Connection details** — How the enclosure attaches to your home

**Engineering fees typically add $500–$1,500** to your project cost, but they're non-negotiable in HVHZ areas.

### 3. Permit Requirements

Screen enclosures are not exempt from permitting in South Florida.

**Required permits:**

- Building permit (structural)
- Possibly electrical permit (if adding lighting or fans)
- HOA approval (if applicable)

**Permit costs: $300–$800+** depending on project scope and municipality.

### 4. Higher Material and Labor Costs

South Florida pricing reflects:

- Higher cost of living
- Demand from constant hurricane repairs
- Specialized HVHZ-compliant materials
- Skilled labor familiar with local codes

**This is not a DIY project** — and going with the cheapest contractor usually means they're cutting corners.

## Factors That Affect Screen Enclosure Pricing

Let's break down what drives the cost of your project.

### 1. Size and Square Footage

This is the most obvious factor.

**Cost per square foot typically ranges:**

- **Standard aluminum frame:** $10–$15 per sq ft installed
- **Premium aluminum or steel frame:** $15–$25 per sq ft installed
- **Rescreening only:** $2–$4 per sq ft

A 1,000 sq ft pool enclosure at $12 per sq ft = $12,000 before engineering and permits.

### 2. Frame Material

**Aluminum Frames**

- **Most common** in South Florida
- Rust-resistant and lightweight
- Corrosion-resistant (critical near salt water)
- Powder-coated finish options
- **Cost:** $8–$15 per sq ft installed

**Steel Frames**

- **Strongest option** — Better for large spans
- Requires proper coating to prevent rust
- Often required for commercial or oversized residential
- **Cost:** $12–$20+ per sq ft installed

**Aluminum is the standard choice** for residential pool enclosures in South Florida — it's proven, durable, and code-compliant.

### 3. Screen Type

Not all screen is created equal.

**Standard Fiberglass Screen**

- Most affordable option
- Good visibility
- Basic bug protection
- **Lifespan:** 7–10 years in South Florida sun
- **Cost:** Lowest tier

**Pet-Resistant Screen**

- Thicker, more durable mesh
- Resists claws and chewing
- Slightly reduced visibility
- **Cost:** 15–25% premium over standard

**Nano-Mesh Screen**

- Fine weave blocks no-see-ums and fine debris
- More opaque appearance
- Better for coastal properties
- **Cost:** 20–30% premium

**Solar Screen**

- Blocks UV and reduces heat
- Extends furniture life
- Darkens view slightly
- **Cost:** 30–50% premium

**For most South Florida pool enclosures, standard fiberglass or pet-resistant screen is the sweet spot** between cost and performance.

### 4. Height and Complexity

**Standard height:** 10–12 feet at peak

**Cathedral/vaulted ceiling:** 14–18 feet at peak

Higher enclosures require:

- More structural support
- Additional bracing
- More expensive engineering
- Increased labor costs

**Complex roof lines** (multiple peaks, valleys, hip roofs) also increase costs significantly.

### 5. Attached vs Freestanding

**Attached Enclosures** (connected to house):

- Require proper flashing and waterproofing
- Must integrate with existing structure
- May require modifications to home
- **Cost:** Moderate

**Freestanding Enclosures** (separate structure):

- Require full structural support on all sides
- More posts and footings
- Simplified construction in some ways
- **Cost:** Can be lower or higher depending on size

### 6. Pool Safety Requirements

Florida law requires **pool barriers** to prevent accidental drowning.

If your screen enclosure serves as the pool barrier, it must meet:

- **Self-closing, self-latching gates**
- **Minimum height requirements** (4 feet)
- **Mesh opening specifications** (no gaps larger than 1¾")
- **Proper spacing between ground and screen**

These requirements are built into most pool enclosure designs, but confirm with your contractor.

## HVHZ Screen Enclosure Requirements

Here's what makes South Florida screen enclosures different from the rest of the state.

### Wind Load Requirements

**HVHZ areas must design for:**

- **180 mph wind speeds** (3-second gust)
- **Wind-borne debris impact** in certain zones
- **Uplift resistance** — Enclosures must resist being lifted off

This requires:

✓ Engineered aluminum extrusions (not standard profiles)

✓ Reinforced post-to-footing connections

✓ Proper lateral bracing

✓ Heavier gauge materials

✓ Specific fastener spacing and types

### Permitting in HVHZ Areas

Expect the permit process to include:

1. **Engineered drawings** — Stamped by Florida PE
2. **Wind load calculations** — Site-specific engineering
3. **Foundation inspection** — Before posts are set
4. **Frame inspection** — Before screening
5. **Final inspection** — After completion

**Timeline:** Permits can take 2–6 weeks depending on the municipality.

**If your contractor suggests skipping permits or engineering, walk away.** Unpermitted screen enclosures can:

- Void your homeowner's insurance
- Create liability if the structure fails
- Cause problems when selling your home
- Result in fines and forced removal

## Rescreening vs Full Rebuild: Which Do You Need?

Not every damaged screen enclosure needs full replacement.

### When Rescreening Makes Sense

✓ **Aluminum frame is structurally sound** — No cracks, bends, or corrosion

✓ **Screen is the only damage** — Tears, holes, or UV deterioration

✓ **Frame meets current code** — Older enclosures may not

✓ **Budget-conscious** — Rescreening costs 70–80% less than full rebuild

**Cost:** $2–$4 per square foot = $2,000–$4,000 for a typical 1,000 sq ft pool cage

### When You Need a Full Rebuild

✗ **Frame is corroded or damaged** — Especially at post bases

✗ **Structure failed in hurricane** — Likely doesn't meet current code

✗ **Built before current HVHZ standards** — Pre-2002 enclosures may not comply

✗ **Rust or structural issues** — Painting over rust doesn't fix it

✗ **Want to change size or configuration** — Adding height or expanding footprint

**Cost:** $10,000–$25,000+ depending on size and materials

### How to Know Which You Need

Have a licensed contractor perform an inspection. They should:

- Check frame for structural integrity
- Look for corrosion at connections
- Verify code compliance
- Provide honest recommendation

**Be wary of contractors who always recommend full replacement** — get a second opinion if rescreening might work.

## Pool Enclosure Specific Considerations

Pool enclosures have unique requirements beyond standard screen rooms.

### Required Features

**1. Self-Closing, Self-Latching Gates**

- Required by Florida law for pool barriers
- Must close and latch automatically
- No override or hold-open features
- Add $300–$600 per gate

**2. Proper Screen Specifications**

- Mesh openings no larger than 1¾"
- Prevents small children from passing through
- Standard fiberglass screen meets this requirement

**3. Minimum Height Requirements**

- Barrier must be at least 4 feet high
- Most pool enclosures are 10–12 feet at peak (compliant)

**4. No Climbable Features**

- Horizontal bars or ledges create climbing hazards
- Must be designed to prevent easy climbing

### Insurance Considerations

Many insurance companies in Florida:

- **Offer discounts** for properly permitted pool enclosures
- **Require enclosures** for pool coverage in some areas
- **May deny claims** if enclosure isn't code-compliant

**Always confirm your enclosure meets both building code and insurance requirements.**

## Screen Enclosure vs Lanai: What's the Difference?

You'll hear both terms in South Florida.

**Screen Enclosure / Pool Cage:**

- Open aluminum frame structure

## Permit Process for Screen Enclosures

Here's what to expect when permitting a screen enclosure in South Florida.

### Step 1: Engineering

Your contractor hires a Florida-licensed Professional Engineer (PE) to create:

- Structural drawings
- Wind load calculations
- Foundation specifications
- Connection details

**Timeline:** 1–2 weeks

**Cost:** $500–$1,500

### Step 2: Permit Application

Contractor submits:

- Engineering drawings
- Plot plan showing enclosure location
- Contractor's license and insurance
- HOA approval (if required)

**Timeline:** 2–4 weeks for approval

**Cost:** $300–$800 depending on municipality

### Step 3: Construction

Once permitted, construction begins:

1. **Foundation inspection** — After footings poured, before posts
2. **Frame inspection** — Before screening installed
3. **Final inspection** — After completion

### Step 4: Final Approval

Building department signs off. You receive:

- Certificate of Completion
- As-built drawings
- Permit closure documentation

**Keep these documents.** You'll need them for insurance, home sales, and future work.

## Screen Enclosure Maintenance and Lifespan

Understanding long-term costs helps you budget properly.

### Expected Lifespan

**Aluminum Frame:** 20–30+ years with proper maintenance

**Screen Mesh:** 7–12 years in South Florida sun and salt air

**Fasteners and Hardware:** 15–20 years

### Maintenance Costs

**Annual Inspections:** $0 (DIY) — Look for screen damage, loose screws, corrosion

**Rescreening:** Every 8–10 years = $2,000–$4,000

**Hardware Replacement:** Occasional = $100–$300

### Hurricane Preparedness

Unlike hurricane shutters or panels, **screen enclosures are designed to remain in place during hurricanes**.

However, you should:

- Remove loose furniture and items
- Inspect for damage after storms
- File insurance claims promptly if damaged
- Document pre-existing condition with photos

**Properly engineered HVHZ enclosures have survived Category 4 hurricanes.** Cheap, unpermitted structures don't.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does a pool screen enclosure cost in Broward County?

A typical pool screen enclosure in Broward County costs $10,000–$20,000 for a standard 1,000–1,500 square foot installation. Broward County is a High Velocity Hurricane Zone (HVHZ), which requires engineered structures designed for 180mph winds — this adds $1,500–$3,000 compared to non-HVHZ areas. Factors affecting cost include size, frame material (aluminum vs steel), screen type, height, and complexity. Engineering fees ($500–$1,500) and permits ($300–$800) are required and should be included in any legitimate quote.

### Do I need a permit for a screen enclosure in Palm Beach County?

Yes, all screen enclosures in Palm Beach County require a building permit. Since most of Palm Beach County is designated HVHZ (High Velocity Hurricane Zone), you'll also need engineered drawings stamped by a Florida-licensed Professional Engineer. The permit process typically takes 2–4 weeks and costs $300–$800 depending on project size and municipality. Any contractor suggesting you skip permits is asking you to break the law and void your homeowner's insurance — this creates massive liability and can force removal of the structure.

### How long do screen enclosures last in South Florida?

Aluminum frames in South Florida typically last 20–30+ years with proper maintenance, while screen mesh lasts 7–12 years before needing replacement due to UV exposure and salt air. The lifespan depends on material quality, installation quality, proximity to salt water, and maintenance. Rescreening costs $2–$4 per square foot ($2,000–$4,000 for a typical pool cage) and is a normal maintenance expense every 8–10 years. Properly engineered and permitted HVHZ-compliant enclosures have survived multiple Category 4 hurricanes, while cheap unpermitted structures often fail in tropical storms.

### What is the difference between HVHZ and non-HVHZ screen enclosures?

HVHZ (High Velocity Hurricane Zone) screen enclosures must be engineered to withstand 180mph winds versus 140mph in non-HVHZ areas. This requires heavier gauge aluminum, enhanced fastening, reinforced post connections, engineered drawings stamped by a Florida PE, and stricter inspections. Most of Broward and Palm Beach Counties are HVHZ, which adds approximately 20–30% to project costs compared to Central or North Florida. The engineering requirement alone adds $500–$1,500, plus higher material costs for compliant extrusions and fasteners. Attempting to use non-HVHZ materials or methods in HVHZ areas is a code violation that won't pass inspection.

### Can I rescreen my existing pool cage or do I need full replacement?

If your aluminum frame is structurally sound with no cracks, bends, or significant corrosion, rescreening is a cost-effective option at $2–$4 per square foot ($2,000–$4,000 for typical pool cage) versus $10,000–$25,000 for full replacement. However, you need full replacement if: the frame is corroded or damaged (especially at post bases), the structure failed during a hurricane, it was built before current HVHZ standards (pre-2002), or you want to change the size or configuration. Have a licensed contractor inspect your frame to provide an honest assessment — be wary of contractors who always recommend full replacement when rescreening would work.
